🌏 US ↔ Australia Tax Guide

Key tax considerations for US citizens moving to Australia in retirement.

This is general educational information only, not tax advice. Cross-border taxation between the US and Australia is genuinely complex. Engage a tax professional who holds qualifications in both jurisdictions — this combination is uncommon, so ask explicitly.

The Core Problem: Citizenship-Based Taxation

The US is one of only two countries (with Eritrea) that taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. This means even after you move to Australia and pay Australian taxes, you must still file and potentially pay US taxes on all income — Australian salary, Super earnings, rental income, and more.

Australia, like most countries, uses residency-based taxation — once you become a tax resident, you pay Australian tax on your worldwide income too.

The result: potential double taxation, mitigated by treaty and credits.

The US-Australia Tax Treaty

The US and Australia have a tax treaty (1982, updated 2001) that helps, but doesn't eliminate all conflicts. Key points:

  • Provides relief from double taxation via credits
  • Does not fully resolve Super treatment
  • Does not include a "savings clause" carve-out for all retirement accounts
  • Covers Social Security under the Totalization Agreement (separate treaty)

Account Treatment by Type

🇺🇸 US Accounts

  • 401(k)/IRA: Australia generally respects these as pension funds; growth may be deferred for AU purposes with a treaty election
  • Roth IRA: Tax-free in US; ATO may tax Roth earnings annually if no treaty election made
  • Brokerage: Gains taxable in both countries; Foreign Tax Credit offsets US tax

🇦🇺 Australian Super

  • IRS view: Foreign grantor trust — earnings taxable annually in US
  • ATO view: Normal super — 15% in accumulation, 0% after 60
  • Conflict: The IRS and ATO treat Super differently — creates significant filing complexity
  • Relief: Some treaty elections available; check with specialist

Social Security & Age Pension

US Social Security

The US-Australia Totalization Agreement means time worked in Australia can count toward US Social Security eligibility (and vice versa). You cannot receive both based on the same credits, but each country pays its own portion.

As an Australian resident, up to 85% of your Social Security benefit may still be subject to US federal income tax. Australia also may tax Social Security income (though often reduced by treaty).

Australian Age Pension

The Australian Age Pension (currently eligible from age 67) is means and assets tested. If you have significant retirement savings, you may receive a partial or no pension. As a US citizen, your worldwide assets count in the assets test.

Note: Age Pension is not currently modeled in this tool but is planned for a future update.

Capital Gains: The Departure Tax Risk

⚠️ When you leave Australia and cease being a tax resident, Australia's "deemed disposal" rules may trigger capital gains on certain assets as if you sold them on the day you left — even if you didn't actually sell. This is important for those who may eventually return to the US.

Similarly, certain US assets may be subject to the Mark-to-Market election (Section 877A "exit tax") if you ever relinquish US citizenship — though simply moving to Australia does not trigger this.

Foreign Account Reporting

As a US citizen living in Australia, you will have ongoing reporting obligations:

  • FBAR (FinCEN 114): Required if aggregate foreign account value exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year
  • FATCA Form 8938: Required if foreign assets exceed $200K (overseas filers, MFJ)
  • Form 3520: May be required annually for Australian Super (foreign trust)
  • Form 8621: May be required for foreign mutual funds (PFIC treatment)

💡 Many common Australian investment funds (index funds, ETFs) are classified as Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) by the IRS and subject to punitive tax treatment. US citizens in Australia typically need to invest through specially structured accounts or US-listed ETFs.

Practical Planning Tips

  • Maximize Roth conversions before moving to Australia (lower US rates while working)
  • Consider whether to keep contributing to Super vs. brokerage given the dual-filing complexity
  • Sell highly appreciated US assets before establishing Australian tax residency if feasible
  • Maintain records of cost basis for all accounts — critical for both IRS and ATO
  • Engage a tax professional 1–2 years before the planned move date, not after
  • Consider the impact on Medicare premiums (IRMAA) from large income events

Key Professionals to Engage

  • US/AU Tax Attorney or CPA: Someone with dual qualification or experience in both systems (search for "US expat tax Australia")
  • Australian Financial Planner: For Super and Age Pension strategy
  • Estate Planning Attorney: Wills and trusts work differently across borders
